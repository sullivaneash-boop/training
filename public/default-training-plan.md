# IRONMAN 70.3 North Carolina — 21-Week Training Plan

**Race day:** Saturday, October 17, 2026 · Wilmington, NC
**Plan start:** Week of May 25, 2026 (~21 weeks out)
**Built for:** strength-base athlete, first 70.3, ~6–8 training hrs/week, shoulder-aware swim ramp

---

## The course, and what it means for training

| Leg | Distance | Profile | Training implication |
|-----|----------|---------|----------------------|
| **Swim** | 1.2 mi | Banks Channel, saltwater, **incoming-tide current pushing you along**, wetsuit-legal (upper 60s–low 70s water) | The current is a gift. Train for *survival + sighting*, not speed. Wetsuit buoyancy also offloads the shoulder — use it. |
| **Bike** | 56 mi | Flat, ~720 ft total climbing, fast rural roads | This is a **sustained-power** race, not a climbing one. The danger is going too hard because it feels easy. Train pacing discipline. |
| **Run** | 13.1 mi | Flat single loop, downtown Wilmington | Pure aerobic endurance + **run economy off the bike**. The brick is where the race is won or lost. |

**Bottom line:** flat-and-fast favors your build. The two things that'll cost you time are (1) an inefficient, panicked swim and (2) blowing up the run because you hammered the bike. The whole plan is engineered around fixing those two.

---

## How the 21 weeks are structured

| Phase | Weeks | Goal |
|-------|-------|------|
| **1 — Base** | 1–6 | Build aerobic frequency, lock in swim technique, get your body used to 6 days/week. Easy and boring on purpose. |
| **2 — Build** | 7–14 | Add volume + the first real intensity (tempo/threshold). Introduce bricks. This is the engine-building block. |
| **3 — Peak** | 15–18 | Race-specific work: race-pace efforts, open water, the long brick, a full race simulation. |
| **4 — Taper** | 19–21 | Pull volume down hard, keep intensity sharp, arrive fresh. Resist the urge to "cram." |

Every 3rd–4th week is a **deload** (cut ~30% volume) so you absorb the work instead of grinding into a hole. With everything else on your plate, recovery *is* training — skipping deloads is how time-crunched athletes get hurt or burned out.

## What a week actually looks like (micro view)

Six days on, one full rest day (I'd put rest on your busiest work day). Here's the template per phase — adjust which day is which around your schedule.

### Base phase week (example)
- **Mon** — Swim technique 30–40 min + easy strength (full body)
- **Tue** — Easy run 30–40 min (zone 2, conversational)
- **Wed** — Swim 30–40 min (drills + short intervals)
- **Thu** — Bike 45–60 min easy + core
- **Fri** — Rest (or 20-min mobility/walk Lylah)
- **Sat** — Long ride (build per table) + 10-min easy jog off the bike
- **Sun** — Long run + swim if you have it in you

### Build phase week (example)
- **Mon** — Swim (technique + intervals) + lower-body strength
- **Tue** — Bike intervals (threshold) 60–75 min
- **Wed** — Run tempo 45–60 min + core
- **Thu** — Swim 40–45 min (continuous endurance sets)
- **Fri** — Rest
- **Sat** — Long ride + **brick run** (10–25 min off the bike)
- **Sun** — Long run

### Peak phase week (example)
- **Mon** — Swim race-pace sets + maintenance strength (light)
- **Tue** — Bike race-pace intervals 75 min
- **Wed** — Run with race-pace finish 60 min
- **Thu** — Open-water swim if accessible, else pool endurance
- **Fri** — Rest
- **Sat** — Long ride + long brick run
- **Sun** — Long run

## The swim — your highest-leverage work (and the shoulder)

You'll get the biggest race-day return per hour here, *and* it's the leg where your build works against you. Big lats and a previously-injured shoulder mean freestyle volume has to be earned, not rushed.

**Rules:**
1. **Technique before yardage, always.** Weeks 1–6 are mostly drills: catch-up, single-arm, fingertip drag, 6-kick switch. The goal is body position and rotation so your **core and hips** drive the stroke, not your shoulder.
2. **Bilateral breathing** from day one — it balances the load across both shoulders and you'll need both sides for sighting/current in the channel.
3. **Distribute yardage over more, shorter sessions** rather than one big grind. 3×30–40 min beats 1×90 min for shoulder durability.
4. **Any sharp shoulder pain = stop the set.** Pulling/aching is normal load adaptation; sharp/pinching is not. Pull-buoy work (legs up, no kick) can keep you swimming while offloading if a session feels tweaky — but if it's recurring, get it looked at before pushing through.
5. **Wetsuit is your friend** — practice in it at least 3–4 times before race day. The buoyancy lifts your hips and reduces shoulder strain, and you don't want race day to be the first time you feel that squeeze across the chest.
6. **Open water 2–3× minimum** before the race — sighting every 6–8 strokes, swimming straight without lane lines, and the feel of a wetsuit + current. The channel current is *with* you, so the skill is staying relaxed and straight, not fighting.

## Keeping the iron — strength integration

You're not going to stop lifting, and you shouldn't. Strength keeps you durable, protects the shoulder and knees under run volume, and it's part of who you are. But it gets **periodized down**, not maintained at full bore — heavy hypertrophy volume and big endurance weeks compete for the same recovery, and endurance wins the priority fight for this goal.

| Phase | Lifting | Emphasis |
|-------|---------|----------|
| Base | 2× full body | Strength-focused, moderate volume. Posterior chain, single-leg, core, light upper. |
| Build | 2× → 1–2× | Trim total volume. Keep heavy-ish lower body (it makes you a better cyclist), reduce pressing volume that competes with swim recovery. |
| Peak | 1× maintenance | One short session. Just enough to hold strength, not build. Power/explosive over grind. |
| Taper | 1× very light or skip | Movement quality only. No soreness into race week. |

**Sequencing:** lift on your *hard* aerobic days (keep easy days easy), and lift *after* the key swim/bike/run session, never before. Hard days hard, easy days easy — don't let a leg day quietly sabotage your long ride.

## Bricks — the secret weapon

A "brick" is bike-then-run back to back. Running on legs that just cycled 56 miles feels alien the first time — your job is to make it familiar.

- **First brick (Wk 7):** ride your normal long ride, then run 10 min easy immediately after. That's it. Just feel the jelly-legs.
- **Build through:** extend the run portion (10 → 25 min).
- **Big brick (Wk 16–17):** ~45 mi ride + 20–30 min run at race effort. This is the single most race-specific session you'll do.

Practice the **transition** itself a few times — bike racked, helmet off, shoes swapped, out running. Smooth transitions are free time and they calm the nerves.

## Race simulation (Week 17)

One dress rehearsal, ideally on a weekend you can mimic race morning:
- Eat your planned race breakfast 2–3 hrs before.
- ~30-min open-water swim in the wetsuit → ~50 mi ride at *goal bike effort* (not all-out) → ~30–40 min run off the bike.
- Use your exact race-day nutrition and hydration. **Practice fueling on the bike**: aim for ~60–90 g carbs/hr and figure out what your gut tolerates *now*, not on race day.
- Wear/test everything: kit, shoes, sunglasses, watch, nutrition setup.

If something's going to go wrong — chafing, GI issues, a gel you hate — you want to find out here.

## Race-week & race-day notes

**Taper truth:** the fitness is already in the bank by Week 18. The taper makes you *fresh*, not fitter. Trust it. Most first-timers sabotage themselves with panic training in the last 10 days.

**Pacing the bike:** the flat, fast course will tempt you to crush it. Don't. Ride at an effort you could hold a conversation at for the first half, slightly build the second half. **Negative-split the bike and you'll run; blow the bike and you'll walk.**

**The run:** start *slower* than feels right for the first 2 miles — your legs are lying to you off the bike. Then settle into rhythm. Use the "hot corner" crowd energy but don't let it bait you into surging early.

**Conditions:** mid-October Wilmington is mild (highs around 68°F). Comfortable for racing, but it means less heat to force hydration — drink to a plan, not just to thirst. Saltwater swim, so a little extra sodium in your fuel isn't a bad idea.

**Logistics to lock early:** point-to-point course means transition bags and bike check-in the day before. Read the athlete guide twice. Know where T1 and T2 are.

## Gear / setup checklist
- **Bike fit** — get one early in the plan. A proper fit on a road or tri bike saves watts and your back/shoulders over 56 mi.
- **Wetsuit** — train in the actual one you'll race in.
- **Nutrition dialed** — gels/drink mix you've tested, mounted and accessible on the bike.
- **GPS watch** — for pacing by effort/HR, especially to hold yourself back on the bike.
- **Two of everything wearable** for race day (goggles, etc.) — redundancy is cheap insurance.

---

## When to back off (non-negotiable)
- Sharp or recurring shoulder pain on the swim → stop, reassess, see a PT before pushing.
- Resting HR elevated 5–7+ bpm for several days, or you're sleeping badly and dreading sessions → take an extra rest day. You won't lose fitness from one day off; you'll lose weeks from one injury.
- Any run/knee pain that changes your gait → walk it in, don't grind.

You hold yourself to a high standard, which is exactly why the discipline here is knowing when *not* to train. Consistency over 21 weeks beats heroics in any single one.
